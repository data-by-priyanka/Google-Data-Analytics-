## The Six Phases of the Data Analysis Process

Data analysis follows a structured process. Breaking it into clear steps makes complex problems easier to manage.

### 1. Ask

You cannot solve a problem if you don’t clearly understand it.

In this phase:
- Define the problem clearly
- Understand stakeholder expectations
- Focus only on the real issue
- Communicate openly with stakeholders
- Understand the full business context

Ask yourself:
- What problem are stakeholders trying to solve?
- How can data help answer their questions?

---

### 2. Prepare

In this phase, you decide what data is needed and how to collect it.

This includes:
- Identifying important metrics
- Locating data sources
- Ensuring data security
- Organizing data properly

Ask yourself:
- What information do I need?
- What research is required?

---

### 3. Process

This stage focuses on cleaning the data.

Clean data improves accuracy and reliability.

You may:
- Remove duplicates
- Fix formatting issues
- Correct incorrect entries
- Check for bias
- Standardize values

Ask yourself:
- What errors might affect my analysis?
- How can I improve consistency in the data?

---

### 4. Analyze

Here, you examine the data to find insights.

You might:
- Perform calculations
- Combine datasets
- Create summary tables
- Identify trends or patterns

Ask yourself:
- What story is the data telling?
- How does this help solve the business problem?

---

### 5. Share

This phase focuses on communicating your findings clearly.

You should:
- Use charts and dashboards
- Highlight key insights
- Keep explanations simple
- Tailor the presentation to stakeholders

Ask yourself:
- Is this easy to understand?
- Would this make sense if I were the audience?

---

### 6. Act

In the final phase, insights are used to make decisions.

This may involve:
- Providing recommendations
- Adjusting strategies
- Implementing changes

Ask yourself:
- How can stakeholders use these insights?
- What improvements can be made?

---

## Structured Thinking

Structured thinking means breaking a large problem into smaller, manageable steps.

It involves:
- Recognizing the problem
- Organizing information
- Identifying gaps
- Exploring possible solutions

---

## Six Common Types of Data Problems

Data analysts often work with these types of problems:

- **Making predictions** – Estimating future outcomes using data.
- **Categorizing information** – Grouping items based on shared characteristics.
- **Spotting unusual patterns** – Identifying outliers or anomalies.
- **Identifying themes** – Grouping categories into broader ideas.
- **Discovering connections** – Finding relationships between data points.
- **Finding patterns** – Using past data to understand trends.

Note: Categorizing groups items, while identifying themes combines those groups into broader insights.

## Questions to Avoid

### Leading Questions
Questions that push someone toward a specific answer.

Example:  
"These are the best sandwiches, aren’t they?"

---

### Closed-Ended Questions
Questions that can only be answered with yes or no.

Example:  
"Were you satisfied with the service?"

---

### Vague Questions
Questions that lack clarity or context.

Example:  
"Does the tool work?"

---

## Asking Better Questions: SMART Method

Effective questions follow the SMART framework:

- **Specific** – Focused and clearly defined.
- **Measurable** – Can be quantified.
- **Action-oriented** – Leads to actionable outcomes.
- **Relevant** – Related to the actual business problem.
- **Time-bound** – Clearly defines the time period.

---

## Fairness in Questioning

Questions must be fair and unbiased.

Fair questions:
- Avoid assumptions
- Are clear and simple
- Apply to everyone equally
- Do not reinforce bias

Open-ended questions are often better because they allow detailed responses and deeper insights.

---

## Key Takeaway

The quality of your analysis depends on the quality of your questions.

Clear, specific, and fair questions lead to better data, stronger insights, and better business decisions.
