## Bias

Human brains are designed to make quick judgments. This helps us move fast, but it can also lead to bias.

**Bias** is a preference for or against a person, group, or idea.  
It can be conscious or unconscious.

Recognizing that bias exists is the first step toward managing it.

---

## Data Bias

Data bias is a systematic error that skews results in a particular direction.

### Types of Data Bias

**Sampling Bias**  
Occurs when a sample does not accurately represent the population.

How to reduce it:
- Use random sampling
- Ensure all groups have equal chances of selection
- Use visualizations to check for imbalance

Unbiased sampling produces results that better reflect reality.

---

**Observer Bias**  
When different people interpret or observe information differently.

---

**Interpretation Bias**  
When ambiguous data is consistently interpreted in a positive or negative way.

---

**Confirmation Bias**  
When people search for or interpret information that supports their existing beliefs.

Good analysts actively question their assumptions.

---

## Identifying Good Data (ROCCC Framework)

To evaluate data quality, use the ROCCC framework:

- **Reliable** – Comes from trustworthy sources.
- **Original** – Verified with the original source.
- **Comprehensive** – Contains all necessary information.
- **Current** – Up-to-date and relevant.
- **Cited** – Properly referenced and documented.

Avoid poor-quality data. Strong conclusions require strong sources.

# Data Ethics

## Ethics

Ethics refers to standards of right and wrong that guide behavior.

---

## Data Ethics

Data ethics focuses on how data is:
- Collected
- Shared
- Used

It ensures fairness, transparency, and respect for individuals.

---

## Key Aspects of Data Ethics

**Ownership**  
Individuals own their personal data and should control how it is used.

**Transparency**  
Data processes and algorithms should be explainable.

**Consent**  
People should know how their data will be used before agreeing.

**Currency**  
Individuals should be aware of financial uses of their data.

**Privacy**  
Personal data must be protected from misuse or unauthorized access.

**Openness**  
Data should be accessible when appropriate and legally allowed.

---

## Data Anonymization

**Personally Identifiable Information (PII)**  
Information that can identify a person (name, address, ID numbers, etc.).

**Data Anonymization**  
The process of removing or masking identifying information to protect privacy.

Methods include:
- Removing sensitive fields
- Hashing data
- Masking values

**De-identification**  
Completely removing identifying details from a dataset.

Protecting privacy is a core responsibility of data analysts.

---

# Open Data

For data to be considered open, it must meet three standards:

1. **Availability and Access**  
   Data must be easily downloadable and usable.

2. **Reuse and Redistribution**  
   Data must allow sharing and combination with other datasets.

3. **Universal Participation**  
   No restrictions based on person, group, or field of study.

---

## Data Interoperability

Interoperability means systems can:
- Connect
- Share
- Understand each other’s data

Common formats and consistent terminology improve interoperability.

Open data becomes more powerful when it can be combined with other reliable datasets.

---

# Platforms for Public Data

Online platforms allow analysts to:
- Search datasets
- Upload their own data
- Conduct research
- Share projects

For example, platforms like Kaggle allow collaboration and dataset exploration.

---

## Key Takeaway

Responsible data analysis requires:

- Awareness of bias
- Evaluation of data quality
- Ethical data usage
- Protection of privacy
- Careful source verification

Technical skills are important.  
But integrity is what makes an analyst trustworthy.
