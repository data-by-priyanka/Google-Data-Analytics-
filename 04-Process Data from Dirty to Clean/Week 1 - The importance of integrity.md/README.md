# Data Integrity

**Data integrity** refers to the accuracy, completeness, consistency, and reliability of data throughout its lifecycle.

If integrity is compromised, conclusions become unreliable.

---

## How Data Integrity Can Be Compromised

Data can lose integrity during:

### Data Replication
Storing data in multiple locations.

Risk:
- Versions can become out of sync
- Teams may use different data versions

---

### Data Transfer
Moving data from one system to another.

Risk:
- Interrupted transfers
- Incomplete datasets

---

### Data Manipulation
Transforming or organizing data for analysis.

Risk:
- Human error during cleaning or formatting
- Incorrect transformations

---

### Other Threats
- Human error
- Malware or viruses
- Hacking
- System failures

---

## Data Integrity and Business Objectives

Accurate conclusions require:

- Clean data
- Alignment with business objectives
- Awareness of new variables
- Understanding constraints

Strong data integrity ensures business decisions are based on trustworthy information.

---

# Insufficient Data

## Types of Insufficient Data

- Data from only one source
- Continuously updating data
- Outdated data
- Geographically limited data

---

## Ways to Address Insufficient Data

- Identify trends using available data
- Wait to collect more data (if possible)
- Adjust the project objective
- Search for alternative datasets

---

## Common Data Issues

### No Data
- Collect small-scale data for preliminary analysis
- Use proxy data from similar datasets

---

### Too Little Data
- Combine proxy data with real data
- Adjust analysis scope

---

### Wrong or Error-Filled Data
- Clarify project requirements
- Identify patterns in errors
- Correct issues at the source
- Exclude incorrect data if it does not cause bias

---

# Random Sampling

**Random sampling** ensures every member of a population has an equal chance of selection.

This improves fairness and reduces sampling bias.

---

# Pre-Cleaning Activities

Pre-cleaning helps maintain data integrity before analysis begins.

Benefits:
- Detect insufficient data early
- Confirm data consistency
- Increase analysis efficiency
- Improve reliability of results

If data is accurate, complete, and aligned with business objectives, results will be more trustworthy.

---

# Statistical Power

**Statistical power** is the probability that a test will detect a true effect.

- Usually expressed as a value between 0 and 1
- A power of 0.8 (80%) is commonly considered acceptable

Higher statistical power increases confidence in results.

---

# Hypothesis Testing

**Hypothesis testing** determines whether results are statistically meaningful.

If results are statistically significant, they are unlikely to be caused by random chance.

---

# Confidence Level

The **confidence level** represents how certain we are that the sample reflects the population.

Common confidence levels:
- 90%
- 95%
- 99%

Higher confidence levels increase reliability but may require larger sample sizes.

---

# Estimated Response Rate

The **estimated response rate** is the percentage of people expected to complete a survey.

This affects how many surveys must be distributed.

---

# Sample Size

A **sample size calculator** helps determine how many observations are required to:

- Achieve a specific confidence level
- Maintain a desired margin of error
- Represent the population accurately

Required inputs:
- Population size
- Confidence level
- Margin of error

---

# Margin of Error

The **margin of error** is the maximum expected difference between sample results and actual population results.

- Smaller margin of error = more precise results
- Larger sample size reduces margin of error
- Lowering confidence level reduces required sample size but reduces certainty

---

# Key Takeaway

Strong data integrity ensures:

- Accurate analysis
- Reliable conclusions
- Better business decisions
- Stakeholder trust

Without integrity, even advanced analysis loses value.
